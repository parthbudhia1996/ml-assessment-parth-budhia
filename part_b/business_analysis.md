# Part B: Business Case Analysis
## Scenario: Promotion Effectiveness at a Fashion Retail Chain

This case study focuses on using Machine Learning to optimize promotional strategies for a fashion retail chain. Here is a comprehensive solution for the business case analysis.       

## B1. Problem Formulation
**(a) Machine Learning Problem Formulation**

1. Target Variable: Total items sold (`items_sold`) at a store-month level (Continuous numeric value).

2. Candidate Input Features:

    Store Attributes: `store_id`, Location-(Urban/Rural)(`location_type`), store size (`store_size`), competition density(`competition_density`), demographic (`demographic`).

    Promotion Details: Promotion type(`promotion_type`), `promo_id`.

    Temporal/External Details: Month (`month`), weekend (`is_weekend`)/festival flags (`is_festival`), monthly footfall (`footfall`).

    Transaction Details: transactions (`transaction_id`), `transaction_date`

    **Final Input Features:** `store_id`, `location_type`, `store_size`, `competition_density`, `promotion_type`, `promo_id`, `month`, `is_weekend`, `is_festival`, `transaction_date`, `transaction_id`

3. Problem Type: This is a Regression problem because we are predicting a continuous numerical value (number of items). Alternatively, if formulated to pick the "best" promotion directly, it could be a Multi-class Classification or a Prescriptive Optimization problem.

4. Justification: Regression is chosen because the business goal is to "maximize the number of items sold." By predicting the volume for each promotion type, we can select the one with the highest predicted value.

**(b) Target Variable Selection: Revenue vs. Volume**

1. Why Volume (`items_sold`) is more reliable: Revenue is heavily influenced by price fluctuations, product mix (selling one expensive jacket vs. ten t-shirts), and the discount depth itself. Sales volume directly measures customer engagement and inventory movement, which is the primary goal of a promotion.

2. Broader Principle: Alignment with Business Objective. In ML, the target variable must be a direct proxy for the behavior you want to influence, minimizing "noise" from external factors (like price changes) that the model isn't intended to solve.

**(c) Alternative Modeling Strategy**

1. Proposed Strategy: Clustered Modeling or Hierarchical/Mixed-Effects Modeling.

2. Justification: Instead of one global model, we can group stores by location type (Urban vs. Rural) and build sub-models, or include Interaction Terms between promotion type and store location. This accounts for the fact that a "Flat Discount" might drive high volume in Rural areas, while "Loyalty Points" might perform better in Urban centers.

## B2. Data and EDA Strategy
**(a) Joining and Aggregation**

1. Joining: Start with the Transactions table as the base. Left Join Store Attributes on `store_id`, Promotion Details on `promo_id`, Calendar on `date` and transactions on `transaction_id`,

2. Grain of Final Dataset: One row = Store-Month-Promotion Type.

3. Aggregations: Sum of items sold per store per month, average footfall per month, and binary flags for whether a festival occurred in that month.   
        - sum(`items_sold`)  
        - avg(`footfall`)  
        - avg(`competition_density`)  
        - bool(`is_festival`)

**(b) EDA Strategy**

Analysis or Charts:
1. Boxplot of Items Sold by Promotion Type: To see which promotion has the highest median sales and identify outliers.

2. Heatmap of Location vs. Promotion: To identify if certain promotions consistently over-perform in specific demographics (e.g., Urban).

3. Time-Series Line Chart: To check for seasonality (e.g., do sales spike in December regardless of the promotion?).

4. Scatter Plot: Store Size va Items Sold to check the influence of physical capacity on sales volume.

5. Correlation Matrix: To see if footfall or competition density has a stronger linear relationship with sales than the promotions themselves.

Influence: If seasonality is high, I will add "Month" as a categorical feature. If competition density is highly correlated with low sales, I might create a "Competitive Pressure" index.

**(c) Handling Data Imbalance**

1. Effect: If 80% of the transactions have no promotion, the model may become biased toward "No Promotion" scenarios, failing to accurately predict the "lift" generated by an actual promotion.

2. Steps to Address: Use Stratified Sampling to ensure the training set has a balanced representation of promotional vs. non-promotional months or Downsampling of the non-promo days or use Evaluation Metrics like MAE/RMSE specifically on the minority (promotion) cases to ensure accuracy where it matters.

3. Feature Engineering: Create a "Control Group" flag to help the model distinguish baseline sales from promotional peaks.

## B3. Model Evaluation and Deployment
**(a) Train-Test Split & Metrics**

1. Split Strategy: Time-based Split (e.g., Train on first 30 months, Test on last 6 months).

2. Why Random Split is inappropriate: Sales data is a time series. Randomly splitting would cause Data Leakage, where the model "sees" the future (e.g., December sales) to predict the past (e.g., November sales).

3. Metrics:  
- MAE (Mean Absolute Error): To understand the average unit error in items sold.
- RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error): To penalize large prediction misses, which are costly for inventory.

**(b) Feature Importance & Communication**

1. Investigation: For Store 12 in December, "Festival Flag" and "Loyalty Points" might have high SHAP values or feature importance, as holiday shoppers seek rewards. In March, "Flat Discount" might show high importance because it's a "low" month where customers are more price-sensitive.

2. Communication: I would tell the marketing team: "In December, the model prioritizes Loyalty because our data shows your high-value customers shop most during the holidays. In March, shoppers are more budget-conscious, so the Flat Discount drives the necessary volume."

**(c) Deployment & Monitoring**

1. Process:
-  Save: Serialize the model using joblib or pickle.
-  Pipeline: Build an automated script that pulls the last month's data, cleans it, and triggers the .predict() function.
-  Feed-in: The predictions are pushed to a dashboard (Tableau/Power BI) on the 1st of every month for store managers.

2. Monitoring:
- Track Model Drift: If the error (MAE) increases over three consecutive months, or if customer demographics shift significantly, the model should trigger an automated retraining alert.
- Performance Decay or MLflow: If the model error (RMSE) increases by >15% over a month, the model should trigger an automated retraining alert.
